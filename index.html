Overall Analysis Questions

  1. Where municipalities fall outside the MBTA Communities Act along outer commuter rail lines, does this create gaps in housing density requirements near transit, and what does that mean for equitable TOD in Metro Boston?

Discoveries & Insights

Motivation

I have personally experienced the challenge of unaffordable housing in Boston, and it is one reason I believe transit-oriented development (TOD) could be one of the most effective solutions. When I was looking for affordable housing, I also needed reliable access to downtown Boston for work. In reality, my viable options were limited to a small geographic radius – areas close enough to make the commute manageable. Unfortunately, those neighborhoods were also among the most expensive. Beyond that radius, housing options became limited and the commute time increased significantly, making them impractical. By contrast, when I was living in London, that effective “commuting radius” felt much larger. Although London is a bigger city than Boston, the extensive and efficient public transportation network meant that more neighborhoods were realistically accessible. As a result, I had far more housing options within a reasonable commute. Transit made distance less of a constraint. As Boston continues to grow, London offers a useful model for how TOD can expand access and opportunity. Developments such as King’s Cross are frequently cited as successful examples of transit-oriented development, where dense housing, jobs, and amenities are integrated with major transit hubs. By investing in similar strategies, Boston could effectively “shrink” commuting distances and open up more affordable housing options across a wider area.

Datasets

Two separate datasets were used to create these plots. The first dataset is a GeoJSON file generated from the MBTA’s GTFS feed. It contains transit route data represented as polygon geometries, with a 40-meter buffer applied around each route to approximate service areas. I focused specifically on the Commuter Rail system, as it is one of the primary ways people travel to Boston for daily work. Because my subtheme is transit-oriented development (TOD), I also wanted to analyze the distribution of single-family housing in the Greater Boston area. The second dataset was created using data from the Metropolitan Area Planning Council (MAPC) Zoning Atlas and the United States Census Bureau. It includes municipal-level information on whether areas are zoned for single-family housing, as well as demographic data such as age distribution and median household income.

Analysis for Questions

The map illustrates the geographic reach of the MBTA Communities Act across Metro Boston's commuter rail network, highlighting a small but significant cluster of non-compliant municipalities (Bolton, Hudson, and Milford) that fall outside the law's coverage. The MBTA Communities Act requires municipalities served by the Massachusetts Bay Transportation Authority to designate at least one multifamily zoning district within 0.5 miles of a commuter rail station, with a minimum gross density of 15 units per acre. Considering that the majority of zones belong to MBTA Communities Act, we would expect more multifamily zoning in general. Let’s investigate this in the next plot. The map shows that single-family zoning covers much of the land along Metro Boston’s commuter rail lines. Towns such as Bolton, Hopkinton, Dover, Duxbury, and Wenham are zoned almost entirely (100%) for single-family homes. This pattern presents a significant barrier to TOD, which depends on denser, mixed-use housing located near transit stations in order to function effectively. In many communities in eastern Massachusetts, areas surrounding commuter rail stations are dominated by large-lot single-family neighborhoods. As a result, land that could otherwise support multifamily or mixed-use development remains unavailable. This issue is particularly visible along the outer commuter rail lines, where restrictive zoning limits housing supply and contributes to higher prices for residents who rely on commuter rail service to access jobs in Boston. However, the continued prevalence of near-total single-family zoning in many commuter rail municipalities suggests that meaningful TOD-supportive reform has yet to be fully realized across the broader Metro Boston region. To better understand the factors underlying these patterns, I compared age distributions between municipalities zoned entirely for single-family housing and those that are not. Using Python, I processed the Census data to calculate the percentage of residents in each age group for both categories. The resulting plot is shown below. Surprisingly, the age distributions were nearly identical across the two groups. I had expected municipalities dominated by single-family zoning to have an older population. However, it is important to note that the Census dataset aggregates everyone between ages 18 and 65 into a single category. This broad grouping likely hides meaningful differences, as it treats a college student and someone nearing retirement as part of the same cohort. The lack of more granular age data may therefore help explain why no clear age-based trend appears in the results.

Summary

In summary, this analysis examined the effectiveness of the MBTA Communities Act and the relationship between the Massachusetts Bay Transportation Authority Commuter Rail system and the distribution of single-family housing across the Metro Boston area. The results show that a substantial share of land near commuter rail lines remains zoned for single-family housing. This pattern suggests that significant structural barriers to transit-oriented development (TOD) present. Overall, the findings indicate that considerable policy and zoning reforms are still needed to enable more effective implementation of TOD throughout the Metro Boston region. .
